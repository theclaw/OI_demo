Architecture Overview

No matter what business youre in, your organization creates massive amounts of data; machine data. Generated by the websites, applications, servers, network devices and mobile devices that power your business. Its one of the fastest growing and most complex parts of Big Data. Without Splunk, this machine data is hard to collect and even harder to make sense of. Splunk makes this machine data accessible, usable and valuable to everyone. Lets see how thats done.

 

Splunk software transforms your machine data into valuable real-time insight. Insight that:

  • deepens understanding of customer behavior,
  • improves service levels,
  • reduces operational costs,
  • and mitigates cyber security risks.

 

Splunk helps you take this machine data and make business sense of it. We call this operational intelligence.

 

With Splunks Universal Machine Data Platform, we are able to capture this information without the use of special connectors or adaptors. We do this real time, any time, so that you have critical information the second it happens. Unlike using database technology, with Splunk there is no need to create predefined schemas, this dramatically reduces implementation costs and provides fast time to value.

 

To help you understand how Splunk might help your organization, we have built a demonstration that represents a large national wireless provider. This demonstration will give you a sense of how different roles within an organization might use Splunk to answer different types of questions, all from the same collection of data. Whether you are an executive, IT Operations manager, support person, or developer, Splunk can help provide the level of detail that you need.

 

 

Background

SplunkTel is a large national wireless provider.  Like most companies, SplunkTel has a desire to monitor in real time the business activity that is flowing through its systems, both to monitor the systems from a stability perspective, and also to provide real time business metrics to its business users. 

 

In addition to monitoring the transactions that flow through their Retail Channel and Web Store, SplunkTel has recently introduced a new music service to improve monetization of their network while delivering a better end customer experience.  The music service enables users to download an unlimited number of songs for a monthly subscription. The company has deployed Splunk to get visibility across these services from a business, customer care and an IT standpoint.

 

Data Sources and Access

The key sources of data for business and IT visibility across these services include:               

                           

      Business Process Management logs

      RADIUS authentication logs

      Web logs from SplunkTel's music service

      Database logs and searches

      Middleware logs

      Geo location data

 

Business Process Management systems are the orchestration systems that make for a really nice visibility point for any enterprise.  Since business logic is encoded in these systems, and they often provide business event based decision processing as well, they provide a great point to capture business data to provide real time insight.

 

RADIUS authentication logs are common in most service providers, ranging from old-style dialup-ISPs, to cable and DSL providers to Wireless and Mobility providers.  RADIUS is a standard, which provides authentication, authorization and accounting, and the providers use it to authenticate their users and assign them profiles (authorize), which determine what they can do on the network, as well as log (account) their usage.

 

The Web logs from SplunkTels music service allow them to see which artists, songs and playlists users are accessing.  These are standard Apache logs.

 

To support their web store, SplunkTel also collects Websphere middleware logs as well as database logs and searches.

Looking at the left, I see Splunk has found an orderType field.  Splunk has auto-extracted this field by examining the data for key/value pairs, such as this=that or this: that.  Splunk will also automatically understand the structure of JSON or XML data. If the data lacks this self-describing structure, Splunk can be configured to extract structure from even the grittiest data by configuring Regular Expression based extractions.  Many of the common data types have been configured for you and these can be downloaded from Splunks website for free.

 

Most importantly, all of this structure is added at search time, meaning if the format of your data changes Splunk will pick up on it automatically or can be easily reconfigured to understand the new structure.  We call this Schema-on-the-fly.  As opposed to the old way of parsing data and throwing it into a rigid structure like a RDBMS, Splunk can easily adapt and understand any data type easily.

<Click Searches & Reporting – OI Music Service – Search Icon >

Before we talk about SplunkTels Customer Care view, lets go back to our search interface to understand a bit more about our machine data.

  

In order to really understand what a user is doing on SplunkTels network they need to track the full session from the time they authenticate into the environment to when they start searching for and downloading music.

 

The challenge with tracking a user through his or her session is that this activity is managed by multiple systems. In this example, the radius authentication logs and the web logs.

 

When a user comes into Splunktel, radius knows their mobile directory number (MDN) and what IP has been assigned to that user at a particular time.  To complicate matters further the IP address will be reused for a different user when this user disconnects. The music logs on the other hand understand time and IP address. <Point out phone number and IP in two different logs>

We need to be able to correlate activity with an understanding of the time an IP Address was assigned to a particular customer. 

 

<Click Searches & Reporting – OI Music Service Transaction– Search Icon >

Using time, MDN, and IP, Splunk is able to tie these events together in the form of a transaction.

 

In this case, what we have is a collection of clustered events representing the activities and actions of that user while they were connected to our service.

 

In fact we can see everything from when they logged in, to what they searched for, and even what they downloaded. We know everything about the behavior of that user during their session. This is where we see the real power of Splunk as a Universal Machine Data platform. We have all the data in one place and regardless of format we are able to combine it in meaningful ways to answer your most challenging business questions.
